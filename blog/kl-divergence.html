Useful KL Divergence Results

The KL divergence between distributions $p(x)$ and $q(x)$ is a measure of dissimilarity, taken as the expectation of the log difference, using $p(x)$ as the weighting function.

For discrete distributions over sample space $\mathcal{X}$, we have: $$ D_{KL}(p||q) = E_{x\sim p(x)}\left[\log\left(\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\right)\right]= \sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x)\log\left(\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\right) \tag{1} $$ whereas for continuous distributions we have the integral: $$D_{KL}(p||q) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} p(x)\log\left(\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\right)dx \tag{2}$$

Properties

  1. If $p$ and $q$ are exactly the same, then $\log p/q = \log(1) = 0$ for all $x$ and $D_{KL}(p||q)=0$. This is the lower bound.

  2. In general the KL divergence is non-negative ($D_{KL} \geq 0$).

    Proof

    If $p=q$, then $D_{KL}=0$ by property 1.

    Otherwise $p \neq q$. Since $\log(a) \leq a-1$ for all $a > 0$, we have:

    $$D_{KL}(p||q)=-\sum p(x) \log \left(\frac{q(x)}{p(x)}\right) \text{(Invert the log)}$$
    $$\geq -\sum p(x)\left(\frac{q(x)}{p(x)} - 1\right) \text{(letting $a=q/p$)}$$
    $$=-\sum q(x) + \sum p(x)$$
    $$=-1 + 1 \text{(probability distributions sum to 1)}$$
    $$=0$$ $$D_{KL} \geq 0$$
